Purpose

We created a tech company website called Quantum that provides software development and test automation solutions to other companies. Our main goal was to create a bold brand identity to connect to companies and clients located anywhere in the world. We created five pages in which we seek to provide a clear picture of what we are as a company and what we can help companies achieve. Since this is a primarily informational website, our focus was to create a language that could transcend geographical and cultural boundaries through a clean design. It took us plenty of investigation and deep exploration on our favorites websites such as Interbrand, Future Brand, Pentagram, Landor, and some more of the tech industry like Huge, Team International, ADK Group, Apple, Miro and so on. The result is a clean website that is not complex since the goal is to not overwhelm the audience with a massive amount of information, but a persuasive site that wants the user to get in contact with us.

Scrum steps using Jira

  1. After creating an account in Atlassian, it is necessary to choose the template in which you will be working within the Jira environment.
  2. Then you must create the project and assign a name to it, it’s called Quatum in our case.
  3. At this time, it is necessary to enter the features/issues that will be developed in the Backlog tab. (In Jira, issues are user stories, tasks, and bugs.)
  4. To create a sprint, it is necessary to do grooming of the tasks to prioritize the issues that are relevant. After the above, we can create the sprint determining its duration and the issues that will be developed.
  5. At the beginning of the sprint, the sprint planning is carried out, in this ceremony, the development team commits to completing a certain number of issues in the sprint.
  6. The issues assigned to each team member can be found in the Board tab, where you can track issues, deadlines, and the status of tickets.
  7. In this way, we ensure that the information is transparent to all members.
  8. The daily meeting is held everyday at a defined time.
  9. During the sprint, it is good to check the Burndown Chart, which shows the actual and estimated amount of work to be done in a sprint. The Burndown Chart is automatically updated by Jira as you complete work items.
  10. To view this chart, click Reports from the sidebar, and then select the Burndown Chart from the reports dropdown.
  11. At the end of the sprint, the review and retrospective meetings are held.
  12. To finish the sprint in Jira, you have to check if there are any incomplete issues, and if there are, move the issues to the backlog, and move them to a future sprint.

Conclusions

  • We completed the website project in five sprints, the first one was a 2-week sprint and the others took us one week each. At the beginning of the project we thought it was convenient to have 2-week sprints but then decided to change the sprint duration to just one week.
  • The average velocity of the team was 18,5 story points per sprint.
  • During the first sprint the team completed the highest amount of story points since the home page had more features that needed to be developed.
  • During the second sprint we only completed 14 story points since the contact us page didn’t have too many features.
  • According to the Cumulative Flow Diagram, most of the time the issues remained in the “To Do” or “Done” status and a very short time in the “In progress” status.
  • An area of opportunity we found was that we forgot to assign the story points for the last three sprints, that’s why the graphs show a horizontal line without any story points. After completing the sprints, Jira allowed us to update the story points, but these changes were not reflected in the graphs. We now know that the story points are fundamental as a way of tracking productivity and generating valuable reports.
